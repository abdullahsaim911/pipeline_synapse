Your teacher is showing this to explain how stem cells maintain their numbers through obligate asymmetric replication and stochastic differentiation.

To visualize this, imagine a 3x3 grid representing various STEM elements in our body. In the top left corner, you can think of a neural cell as an original stem cell. The top center shows 'obligate asymmetric replication', which means when this stem cell divides, it produces one identical mother cell (top left) and one differentiated daughter cell (not shown here). The top right corner represents the red blood cell, a type of differentiated cell produced by the division of a stem cell.

Moving down to the middle sections, we see bone, adipose, and cartilage, which are examples of specialized cells that have developed from stem cells in our bodies. At the bottom of the grid, you'll find the same STEM elements repeated for a clear comparison between different stages or locations.

Now let me tell you about obligate asymmetric replication. When a stem cell divides, it undergoes this process to ensure its numbers stay consistent. Your teacher explains that the mother cell replaces the original stem cell that divided initially, while the daughter cell can become more specialized. This allows for the continuous production of new cells while maintaining a pool of stem cells ready for future division.

The other mechanism mentioned is stochastic differentiation, where if one stem cell happens to differentiate into two daughter cells instead of a mother and a daughter, another stem cell will make up for the loss by undergoing mitosis and producing two new stem cells identical to the original. This ensures that the number of stem cells remains nice and strong.

So, when you look at this grid, remember that each STEM element represents various stages in the lifecycle of a stem cell, demonstrating how these mechanisms help maintain their numbers.
